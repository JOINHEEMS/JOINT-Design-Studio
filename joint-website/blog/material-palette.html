Building a Material Palette

Material selection is one of the most consequential decisions in architectural design. The materials we choose define not only the visual character of a building but also its tactile quality, its relationship to light, and its performance over time.

At Join.T, we approach material palettes with restraint and intention. Rather than deploying a wide range of finishes, we prefer to work with a limited set of carefully selected materials—typically three to five primary materials that can be combined in various ways throughout a project.

Starting with Context

Every material palette begins with an understanding of site and context. What materials are native to the region? What is the vernacular tradition? How does light behave in this location? These questions inform our initial material studies and help establish a connection between the building and its environment.

For the Modern Hillside Home in Livingston, we began with the site's rocky terrain and wooded surroundings. Raw concrete, natural wood, and glass became the foundational materials—each chosen for both aesthetic and functional reasons.

Considering Light and Time

Materials age. This is not a flaw but a feature. We select materials that weather gracefully—that develop patina, that record the passage of time, that become richer rather than merely worn.

"The best materials are those that improve with age, that tell a story, that reveal the hand of the maker and the forces of nature."

Natural wood silvering in the sun, concrete darkening with moisture, stone acquiring moss—these are not defects but expressions of materiality. We design with this transformation in mind, anticipating how a building will look not just at completion but decades later.

Light as Material

Light transforms materials. The same concrete surface reads differently in morning sun versus afternoon shadow. Glass becomes opaque or transparent depending on the angle of view and time of day. We study how light interacts with each material throughout the daily and seasonal cycles.

For the Minimalist Lake House, we positioned the building to capture morning light on warm wood surfaces and afternoon light on cool stone. The material palette was designed specifically for these lighting conditions—each surface calibrated to respond to its particular orientation and exposure.

Craft and Detail

Material selection cannot be separated from questions of craft and detail. How will materials meet? How will they transition from interior to exterior? What reveals or recesses will define these connections?

We spend significant time on these junctions—the place where concrete meets wood, where glass meets stone, where one plane becomes another. These moments are opportunities to express precision and care, to reveal the logic of construction, to demonstrate that materials have been thoughtfully considered at every scale.

Consistency and Variation

A limited material palette doesn't mean monotony. Within the constraint of three or four materials, we find variation through:

  • Different finishes of the same material (honed vs. polished stone)
  • Varying applications (board-formed vs. smooth concrete)
  • Diverse scales (wide-plank wood floors vs. narrow siding)
  • Contrasting orientations (vertical vs. horizontal grain)

This approach creates visual interest while maintaining overall coherence. The palette feels unified yet not repetitive, calm yet not static.

Working with a restrained palette forces clarity in design thinking. Every material must justify its presence.

Sustainability Through Longevity

Perhaps the most sustainable material strategy is to select materials that last. Durable materials that age well, that can be maintained and repaired rather than replaced, that remain beautiful over decades—this is our approach to environmental responsibility.

We favor materials with low embodied energy when possible: locally sourced stone, reclaimed wood, natural plasters. But we also recognize that longevity matters more than initial impact. A building that stands for a century, that continues to function and inspire, is inherently more sustainable than one requiring replacement after twenty years.

Process and Iteration

Building a material palette is an iterative process. We create physical sample boards, study materials in different lights and at different scales, mock up critical junctions. We visit fabricators and suppliers, understanding how materials are made and what variations we can expect.

This process takes time. We might study dozens of stone samples before selecting the right one. We'll test multiple wood stains and sealers. We'll review glass specifications and coating options. The goal is not perfection but appropriateness—finding materials that are right for this project, this site, this client, this budget.

Conclusion

A well-considered material palette is fundamental to architectural quality. It establishes character, ensures coherence, responds to context, and ages gracefully. By working with restraint, by understanding how materials behave over time, and by attending carefully to craft and detail, we create buildings that feel both contemporary and timeless.

The material palette is not decoration applied to architecture—it is architecture itself, expressing structure, enclosing space, modulating light. Every surface matters. Every junction counts. Every material choice is an opportunity to reinforce the clarity and intention of the design.
